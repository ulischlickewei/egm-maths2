Example 1.2.3. Solving a Linear System with an Inverse Matrix.

We illustrate the idea with a simple linear system
\begin{equation*} A\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{v} \end{equation*}
with
\begin{equation*} A= \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1\\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{v}= \begin{pmatrix} 5\\ 3 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
The inverse of \(A\) is
\begin{equation*} A^{-1}= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1\\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
🔗
We postpone the explanation how to compute inverse matrices and simply check that the given matrix is indeed the inverse of \(A\text{.}\) We compute
\begin{equation*} AA^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1\\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1\\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0\\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = I_2. \end{equation*}
Similarly,
\begin{equation*} A^{-1}A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1\\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1\\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0\\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = I_2. \end{equation*}
🔗
Therefore, we may solve the system by multiplying from the left by \(A^{-1}\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} \mathbf{u}=A^{-1}\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1\\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 5\\ 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2\\ 1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
Hence the solution of the linear system is
\begin{equation*} \mathbf{u}= \begin{pmatrix} 2\\ 1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
