    Section 1.1 Brief reminder: vectors

    Definition 1.1.1. Vector and Null Vector.

    A vector of size \(n\) is defined as an ordered tuple of \(n\) real numbers, denoted as \(v_1, \dots, v_n\text{.}\) The set containing all vectors of size \(n\) is represented by \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    A special case is the null vector, which is a vector consisting entirely of zeros and is written as
    \begin{equation*} 0 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.2. Vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

    An example of a vector in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) is \(v = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) The null vector in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) is \(0 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.1.3. Geometric Interpretation.

    Geometrically, vectors can be interpreted as arrows in Euclidean space that possess a direction and a length without a clearly defined starting point, or they can be understood as position vectors that specifically start in the origin.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.4. Vector Operations.

    Vectors can be added together and scaled using real numbers.
    🔗
    • Addition.
      The sum of two vectors \(v\) and \(w\) is determined by component-wise addition, yielding
      \begin{equation*} v+w = \begin{pmatrix} v_1+w_1 \\ \vdots \\ v_n+w_n \end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    • Scalar Multiplication.
      The multiplication of a scalar \(\lambda \in \mathbb{R}\) with a vector \(v\) is given by multiplying each individual component by the scalar, resulting in
      \begin{equation*} \lambda v = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda v_1 \\ \vdots \\ \lambda v_n \end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.5. Calculating Sums and Scalar Multiples.

    Let \(v = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(w = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}\) be vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Their sum is:
    \begin{equation*} v + w = \begin{pmatrix} 1+4 \\ 3+(-2) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    If we scale the vector \(v\) by a scalar \(\lambda = 3\text{,}\) we get:
    \begin{equation*} 3v = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \cdot 1 \\ 3 \cdot 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 9 \end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 1.1.8. Laws of Vector Arithmetic.

    Several important laws of vector arithmetic govern operations for vectors \(u, v, w \in \mathbb{R}^n\) and scalars \(\lambda, \mu \in \mathbb{R}\text{:}\)
    🔗
    • Associativity of Addition: \(u+(v+w)=(u+v)+w\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Commutativity of Addition: \(u+v=v+u\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Neutral Element: The null vector serves as the neutral element, leading to \(u+0=0+u=u\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Inverse Element: Adding a vector to its negative yields the null vector: \(u+(-u)=(-u)+u=0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Left Distributivity: \(\lambda \cdot (u+v) = (\lambda \cdot u) + (\lambda \cdot v)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Right Distributivity: \((\lambda+\mu) \cdot u = (\lambda \cdot u) + (\mu \cdot u)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Associativity of Scalar Multiplication: \((\lambda \cdot \mu) \cdot u = \lambda \cdot (\mu \cdot u)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Multiplication by One: \(1 \cdot u = u\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.9. Linear Independence and Basis.

    The concept of linear independence formalizes the idea of independent geometric directions. Specifically, vectors \(v_1, \dots, v_r \in \mathbb{R}^n\) are considered linearly independent if the equation
    \begin{equation*} x_1 v_1 + \dots + x_r v_r = 0 \end{equation*}
    has only the trivial solution \(x_1 = \dots = x_r = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    A tuple of \(n\) linearly independent vectors \((v_1, \dots, v_n)\) in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is referred to as a basis of \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.10. Standard Basis in \(\mathbb{R}^2\).

    The vectors \(e_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(e_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\) are linearly independent. The equation \(x_1 e_1 + x_2 e_2 = 0\) translates to \(\begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\text{,}\) which clearly forces \(x_1 = 0\) and \(x_2 = 0\text{.}\) Therefore, the tuple \((e_1, e_2)\) forms a basis for \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.11. Scalar Product, Norm, Distance, and Angle.

    To compute lengths of vectors and angles between them, the scalar product is utilized.
    🔗
    • Scalar Product.
      For two vectors \(v\) and \(w\) in \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{,}\) the scalar product is defined as
      \begin{equation*} v \cdot w = v_1 w_1 + \dots + v_n w_n\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    • Norm.
      The norm of a vector \(v\) computes its length and is defined mathematically as
      \begin{equation*} ||v|| = \sqrt{v \cdot v} = \sqrt{v_1^2 + \dots + v_n^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    • Distance.
      The distance between two vectors \(v\) and \(w\) is evaluated as
      \begin{equation*} \text{dist}(v,w) = ||v-w|| = \sqrt{(v_1-w_1)^2 + \dots + (v_n-w_n)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    • Angle.
      The angle \(\varphi\) between the two vectors \(v\) and \(w\) is given by the formula
      \begin{equation*} \varphi = \arccos\left(\frac{v \cdot w}{||v|| ||w||}\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    • Orthogonality.
      Two vectors \(v\) and \(w\) are considered orthogonal if their scalar product evaluates to zero, meaning \(v \cdot w = 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.12. Lengths and Orthogonality.

    Consider the vectors \(v = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(w = \begin{pmatrix} -4 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The norm (length) of \(v\) is:
    \begin{equation*} ||v|| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The scalar product of \(v\) and \(w\) is:
    \begin{equation*} v \cdot w = (3)(-4) + (4)(3) = -12 + 12 = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Because their scalar product is zero, the vectors \(v\) and \(w\) are orthogonal to one another.
    🔗
    🔗
