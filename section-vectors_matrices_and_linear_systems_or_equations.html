    Section 1.1 Vectors, matrices and linear systems of equations

    In this section, we will learn the basic notions underlying linear algebra: vectors, matrices and linear systems and equations. It will turn out, that essentially everything we learn about linear algebra will boil down to these basic concepts.
    🔗

    Subsection 1.1.1 Brief reminder: vectors

    In physics, many real-world quantities—such as force, velocity, or displacement—cannot be fully described by a single number. For example, if you apply a force to push a box, the outcome depends not only on how hard you push (the magnitude) but also on the direction in which you are pushing.
    🔗
    To capture both magnitude and direction simultaneously, we use mathematical objects called vectors. This concept extends naturally beyond physical space into abstract mathematics, allowing us to handle multi-dimensional spaces and systems.
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.1. Vector.

    A vector of size \(n\) is an ordered tuple of \(n\) real numbers. The set of all vectors of size \(n\) is denoted by \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\) A vector \(\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n\) is commonly written as \(\mathbf{v} = (v_1, \dots, v_n)\) or in column form as
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Let us look at a concrete numerical representation of a vector in three-dimensional space.
    🔗

    Example 1.1.2. A Vector in \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

    An example of a vector in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) is
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    In \(\mathbb{R}^2\) and \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{,}\) vectors can be visualized as arrows in Euclidean space. In this geometric interpretation, a vector has a direction and a length, but it is not tied to a fixed starting point. A particularly useful representative is the position vector that starts at the origin.
    🔗
    At the same time, the algebraic point of view is more general: a vector is determined by its components. The geometric picture is very helpful in two and three dimensions, but the coordinate description remains meaningful in arbitrary dimension.
    🔗
    To make vectors useful, we need mathematical rules for how to combine them. The two most fundamental operations are adding two vectors and multiplying a vector by a real number.
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.3. Vector Operations.

    Let \(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n\) and let \(\lambda \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) The sum of two vectors of the same size and the scalar multiple of a vector are defined component-wise.
    🔗
    Addition
    🔗
    The sum of \(\mathbf{v}\) and \(\mathbf{w}\) is
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w} = \begin{pmatrix} v_1+w_1 \\ \vdots \\ v_n+w_n \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Scalar Multiplication
    🔗
    The scalar multiple of \(\mathbf{v}\) by \(\lambda\) is
    \begin{equation*} \lambda \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda v_1 \\ \vdots \\ \lambda v_n \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.4. Calculating Sums and Scalar Multiples.

    Let \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{w} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}\) be vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Their sum is
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w} = \begin{pmatrix} 1+4 \\ 3+(-2) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    If we scale \(\mathbf{v}\) by \(\lambda = 3\text{,}\) we obtain
    \begin{equation*} 3\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \cdot 1 \\ 3 \cdot 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 9 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.5. Resultant Force on a Mechanical Component.

    Suppose two forces act on a component in the plane:
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{F}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 120 \\ 40 \end{pmatrix}\text{ N}, \qquad \mathbf{F}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} -30 \\ 90 \end{pmatrix}\text{ N}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The total force is obtained by vector addition:
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{F}_{\text{res}} = \mathbf{F}_1 + \mathbf{F}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 120 + (-30) \\ 40 + 90 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 90 \\ 130 \end{pmatrix}\text{ N}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Thus, the component experiences a resultant force with horizontal component \(90\text{ N}\) and vertical component \(130\text{ N}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.6. Displacement from a Velocity Vector.

    Consider a mobile robot moving with constant velocity
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 1.2 \\ 0.5 \end{pmatrix}\text{ m/s}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    After \(10\) seconds, its displacement is
    \begin{equation*} 10\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 12 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}\text{ m}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This illustrates the meaning of scalar multiplication: multiplying the velocity vector by the travel time gives the displacement vector.
    🔗
    🔗
    Having established these basic arithmetic operations, a special vector arises naturally when we scale any vector by zero or subtract a vector from itself.
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.9. Zero Vector.

    The zero vector in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is the vector whose components are all equal to zero. It is denoted by \(\mathbf{0}\) and has the form
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{0} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Just like ordinary numbers, vector operations satisfy several basic algebraic laws.
    🔗

    Theorem 1.1.10. Laws of Vector Arithmetic.

    For all vectors \(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n\) and all scalars \(\lambda, \mu \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) the following rules hold.
    🔗
    • Associativity of Addition: \(\mathbf{u}+(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w})=(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v})+\mathbf{w}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Commutativity of Addition: \(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{u}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Neutral Element: \(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{0}=\mathbf{0}+\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{u}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Inverse Element: \(\mathbf{u}+(-\mathbf{u})=(-\mathbf{u})+\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{0}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Left Distributivity: \(\lambda(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v})=\lambda\mathbf{u}+\lambda\mathbf{v}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Right Distributivity: \((\lambda+\mu)\mathbf{u}=\lambda\mathbf{u}+\mu\mathbf{u}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Associativity of Scalar Multiplication: \((\lambda\mu)\mathbf{u}=\lambda(\mu\mathbf{u})\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Multiplication by One: \(1\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{u}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    When working with several vectors, we often want to combine them using scalar multiplication and addition.
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.11. Linear Combination.

    Let \(\mathbf{v}_1, \dots, \mathbf{v}_r \in \mathbb{R}^n\) and let \(x_1, \dots, x_r \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) A vector of the form
    \begin{equation*} x_1 \mathbf{v}_1 + \dots + x_r \mathbf{v}_r \end{equation*}
    is called a linear combination of the vectors \(\mathbf{v}_1, \dots, \mathbf{v}_r\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The concept of linear independence tells us whether a collection of vectors provides genuinely different directions. If one vector can already be obtained as a linear combination of the others, then it does not add new information.
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.13. Linear Independence and Basis.

    Vectors \(\mathbf{v}_1, \dots, \mathbf{v}_r \in \mathbb{R}^n\) are called linearly independent if the equation
    \begin{equation*} x_1 \mathbf{v}_1 + \dots + x_r \mathbf{v}_r = \mathbf{0} \end{equation*}
    has only the trivial solution \(x_1 = \dots = x_r = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    A basis of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is a tuple of \(n\) linearly independent vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\) In that case, every vector in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) can be written uniquely as a linear combination of the basis vectors.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.14. A Counterexample in \(\mathbb{R}^2\).

    Consider the vectors
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since \(\mathbf{v}_2 = 2\mathbf{v}_1\text{,}\) one vector is a scalar multiple of the other. Therefore, the vectors are not linearly independent.
    🔗
    🔗
    The most intuitive example of a basis is given by the standard coordinate axes.
    🔗

    Example 1.1.15. Standard Basis in \(\mathbb{R}^2\).

    The vectors
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{e}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{e}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}
    are linearly independent.
    🔗
    Indeed, the equation
    \begin{equation*} x_1 \mathbf{e}_1 + x_2 \mathbf{e}_2 = \mathbf{0} \end{equation*}
    becomes
    \begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \end{equation*}
    so necessarily \(x_1 = 0\) and \(x_2 = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Hence, \((\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2)\) is a basis of \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\) This means that every vector in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) can be written uniquely as a linear combination of \(\mathbf{e}_1\) and \(\mathbf{e}_2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.16. Three Linearly Independent Vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

    Consider the vectors
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{v}_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    To test linear independence, we consider
    \begin{equation*} x_1\mathbf{v}_1 + x_2\mathbf{v}_2 + x_3\mathbf{v}_3 = \mathbf{0}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This gives
    \begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \end{equation*}
    so \(x_1 = x_2 = x_3 = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Therefore, the three vectors are linearly independent.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.17. A Counterexample with Three Vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

    Consider the vectors
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{v}_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{v}_3 = \mathbf{v}_1 + \mathbf{v}_2, \end{equation*}
    the vector \(\mathbf{v}_3\) is a linear combination of the other two.
    🔗
    Hence, the three vectors are not linearly independent.
    🔗
    🔗
    Besides combining vectors algebraically, we also want to measure geometric quantities such as length, distance, and angle. These are based on the scalar product.
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.18. Scalar Product, Norm, Distance, and Angle.

    Scalar Product
    🔗
    For vectors \(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n\text{,}\) the scalar product is defined by
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{w} = v_1 w_1 + \dots + v_n w_n. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Norm
    🔗
    The norm of \(\mathbf{v}\) is its length:
    \begin{equation*} \|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v}} = \sqrt{v_1^2 + \dots + v_n^2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Distance
    🔗
    The distance between \(\mathbf{v}\) and \(\mathbf{w}\) is
    \begin{equation*} \operatorname{dist}(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}) = \|\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{w}\| = \sqrt{(v_1-w_1)^2 + \dots + (v_n-w_n)^2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Angle
    🔗
    For two non-zero vectors \(\mathbf{v}\) and \(\mathbf{w}\text{,}\) the angle \(\varphi\) between them is defined by
    \begin{equation*} \varphi = \arccos\left(\frac{\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{w}}{\|\mathbf{v}\| \|\mathbf{w}\|}\right). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Orthogonality
    🔗
    Two vectors \(\mathbf{v}\) and \(\mathbf{w}\) are called orthogonal if
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{w} = 0. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    The formula for the norm is a direct generalization of the Pythagorean theorem. In two dimensions, a vector \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \end{pmatrix}\) forms the hypotenuse of a right triangle whose legs have lengths \(|v_1|\) and \(|v_2|\text{.}\) Hence, \(\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2}\text{.}\) The same idea extends to higher dimensions.
    🔗

    Example 1.1.20. Lengths and Orthogonality.

    Consider the vectors \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{w} = \begin{pmatrix} -4 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The norm of \(\mathbf{v}\) is
    \begin{equation*} \|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Their scalar product is
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{w} = (3)(-4) + (4)(3) = -12 + 12 = 0. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since the scalar product is zero, the vectors are orthogonal.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.21. A State Vector for Monitoring a Technical System.

    In engineering applications, vectors are often used to represent system states rather than geometric arrows. For example, the operating condition of a motor might be described by the vector
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} 1.8 \\ 0.3 \\ -0.5 \end{pmatrix}, \end{equation*}
    where the components represent, for instance, a temperature deviation, a vibration level, and a current deviation.
    🔗
    Another operating condition may be represented by
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{y} = \begin{pmatrix} 1.2 \\ 0.1 \\ -0.4 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The distance between the two states is
    \begin{equation*} \operatorname{dist}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) = \|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}\| = \sqrt{(1.8-1.2)^2 + (0.3-0.1)^2 + (-0.5-(-0.4))^2} = \sqrt{0.36 + 0.04 + 0.01} = \sqrt{0.41}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This shows that vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) are not only geometric objects; they can also encode measurements and describe the state of a technical system.
    🔗
    🔗

    Exercises Check Your Understanding

    1. Identifying Orthogonal Vectors.
    Which of the following vectors is orthogonal to \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix}\text{?}\)
    🔗
    • \(\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}\)
    • Incorrect. The scalar product is \((2)(2) + (-3)(3) = 4 - 9 = -5\text{,}\) not zero.
    • \(\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}\)
    • Correct! The scalar product is \((2)(3) + (-3)(2) = 6 - 6 = 0\text{.}\)
    • \(\begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix}\)
    • Incorrect. The scalar product is \((2)(-2) + (-3)(-3) = -4 + 9 = 5\text{,}\) not zero.
    • \(\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\)
    • Incorrect. The scalar product is \((2)(1) + (-3)(1) = 2 - 3 = -1\text{,}\) not zero.
    🔗
    2. Requirements for a Basis.
    True or False: Any two non-zero vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) automatically form a basis of that space.
    🔗
    • True
    • Incorrect. If the two vectors are scalar multiples of one another, then they are not linearly independent and therefore do not form a basis.
    • False
    • Correct! Two vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) form a basis only if they are linearly independent.

    Subsection 1.1.2 Matrices

    Matrices provide a convenient way to organize numerical data in rows and columns. They are used to describe systems of linear equations, to encode linear transformations, and to model input-output relations in engineering, economics, and the natural sciences.
    🔗
    In these notes, vectors are regarded as column vectors. Therefore, a vector in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) can also be viewed as an \((n\times 1)\)-matrix.
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.22. Matrix, Rows, and Columns.

    A matrix is a rectangular array of real numbers arranged in rows and columns. A matrix with \(m\) rows and \(n\) columns is called an \((m\times n)\)-matrix. The set of all real \((m\times n)\)-matrices is denoted by \(\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    A matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\) is written in the form
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{pmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} & \dots & a_{1n} \\ a_{21} & a_{22} & \dots & a_{2n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ a_{m1} & a_{m2} & \dots & a_{mn} \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The entry in the \(i\)-th row and \(j\)-th column is denoted by \(a_{ij}\text{.}\) We also write \(A=(a_{ij})\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The \(i\)-th row of \(A\) is
    \begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} a_{i1} & a_{i2} & \dots & a_{in} \end{pmatrix}, \end{equation*}
    and the \(j\)-th column of \(A\) is
    \begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} a_{1j} \\ a_{2j} \\ \vdots \\ a_{mj} \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.23. An Example of a Matrix.

    The matrix
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 & 4 \\ 0 & 3 & 5 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}
    has \(2\) rows and \(3\) columns, so \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{2\times 3}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Its entry in the first row and third column is \(a_{13}=4\text{.}\) The second row is \(\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 3 & 5 \end{pmatrix}\text{,}\) and the first column is \(\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.24. Equality of Matrices.

    Two matrices \(A\) and \(B\) are equal if they have the same size and if all corresponding entries are equal.
    🔗
    Thus, for \(A=(a_{ij})\) and \(B=(b_{ij})\text{,}\) we have \(A=B\) exactly if \(a_{ij}=b_{ij}\) for all \(i\) and \(j\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsubsection 1.1.2.1 Arithmetic Operations on Matrices

    We now introduce the basic arithmetic operations for matrices.
    🔗
    Definition 1.1.25. Addition and Scalar Multiplication.
    Let \(A=(a_{ij})\) and \(B=(b_{ij})\) be matrices of the same size in \(\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\text{,}\) and let \(\lambda \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The sum of \(A\) and \(B\) is defined component-wise by
    \begin{equation*} A+B = (a_{ij}+b_{ij}). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The scalar multiple of \(A\) by \(\lambda\) is defined by
    \begin{equation*} \lambda A = (\lambda a_{ij}). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Example 1.1.26. Addition and Scalar Multiplication.
    Let
    \begin{equation*} A= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad B= \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 0 \\ 2 & -5 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Then
    \begin{equation*} A+B= \begin{pmatrix} 1+4 & 2+0 \\ -1+2 & 3+(-5) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 2 \\ 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Moreover,
    \begin{equation*} 3A= \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 6 \\ -3 & 9 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Definition 1.1.27. Transpose.
    Let \(A=(a_{ij}) \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\text{.}\) The transpose of \(A\) is the matrix \(A^T \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times m}\) obtained by interchanging rows and columns.
    🔗
    In other words, the entry in row \(j\) and column \(i\) of \(A^T\) is \(a_{ij}\text{.}\) Thus,
    \begin{equation*} (A^T)_{ji}=a_{ij}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Example 1.1.28. Computing a Transpose.
    For
    \begin{equation*} A= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4 & -2 \\ 0 & 3 & 5 \end{pmatrix}, \end{equation*}
    the transpose is
    \begin{equation*} A^T= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 4 & 3 \\ -2 & 5 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Definition 1.1.29. Matrix Multiplication.
    Let \(A=(a_{ij}) \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\) and \(B=(b_{jk}) \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times r}\text{.}\) Then the product \(AB\) is defined, because the number of columns of \(A\) equals the number of rows of \(B\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The product \(AB\) is an \((m\times r)\)-matrix
    \begin{equation*} C=(c_{ik}), \end{equation*}
    whose entries are given by
    \begin{equation*} c_{ik}=a_{i1}b_{1k}+a_{i2}b_{2k}+\dots+a_{in}b_{nk} = \sum_{j=1}^n a_{ij}b_{jk}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Equivalently, the entry \(c_{ik}\) is obtained as the scalar product of the \(i\)-th row of \(A\) and the \(k\)-th column of \(B\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Hence, the product \(AB\) is defined only if the inner dimensions agree: the number of columns of \(A\) must equal the number of rows of \(B\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Example 1.1.30. Computing a Matrix Product.
    Let
    \begin{equation*} A= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad B= \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 1 \\ 6 & 0 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Then
    \begin{equation*} AB= \begin{pmatrix} 1\cdot 5 + 2\cdot 6 & 1\cdot 1 + 2\cdot 0 \\ 3\cdot 5 + 4\cdot 6 & 3\cdot 1 + 4\cdot 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 17 & 1 \\ 39 & 3 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Since vectors are regarded as column vectors, matrix multiplication also allows us to multiply a matrix by a vector.
    🔗
    Definition 1.1.31. Matrix-Vector Product.
    Let \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\) and let \(\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\) Interpreting \(\mathbf{v}\) as an \((n\times 1)\)-matrix, the product \(A\mathbf{v}\) is defined and belongs to \(\mathbb{R}^m\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Example 1.1.32. A Matrix-Vector Product.
    Let
    \begin{equation*} A= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{v}= \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Then
    \begin{equation*} A\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 1\cdot 5 + 2\cdot 6 \\ 3\cdot 5 + 4\cdot 6 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 17 \\ 39 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    The matrix-vector product can also be interpreted in terms of the columns of the matrix. If
    \begin{equation*} A= \begin{pmatrix} \vline & & \vline \\ \mathbf{a}_1 & \dots & \mathbf{a}_n \\ \vline & & \vline \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{v}= \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ \vdots \\ x_n \end{pmatrix}, \end{equation*}
    then
    \begin{equation*} A\mathbf{v}=x_1\mathbf{a}_1+\dots+x_n\mathbf{a}_n. \end{equation*}
    Thus, \(A\mathbf{v}\) is a linear combination of the columns of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Example 1.1.33. Modelling Population Movements.
    Consider a population distributed between suburban areas (\(S\)) and urban areas (\(U\)). Suppose that in one year, \(97\%\) of the suburban population remains in the suburban area and \(3\%\) moves to the urban area. Likewise, \(95\%\) of the urban population remains in the urban area and \(5\%\) moves to the suburban area.
    🔗
    If
    \begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} p_{S,0} \\ p_{U,0} \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}
    denotes the population vector at the beginning of the year, then the population vector at the end of the year is
    \begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} p_{S,1} \\ p_{U,1} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0.97 & 0.05 \\ 0.03 & 0.95 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} p_{S,0} \\ p_{U,0} \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This is an example of a matrix-vector product describing the state update of a system.
    🔗
    🔗
    Example 1.1.34. Sensor Calibration.
    Suppose a sensor system records two raw values collected in the vector
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{x}= \begin{pmatrix} 10 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    A calibration matrix
    \begin{equation*} A= \begin{pmatrix} 1.1 & 0.2 \\ 0.05 & 0.9 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}
    transforms the raw measurements into corrected values:
    \begin{equation*} A\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} 1.1\cdot 10 + 0.2\cdot 4 \\ 0.05\cdot 10 + 0.9\cdot 4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 11.8 \\ 4.1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Thus, matrices can be used to model calibration and coordinate transformations in engineering applications.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsubsection 1.1.2.2 Special Matrices and Laws of Arithmetic

    Definition 1.1.35. Special Matrices.
    Several classes of matrices occur frequently.
    🔗
    Zero Matrix
    🔗
    A matrix all of whose entries are equal to zero.
    🔗
    Square Matrix
    🔗
    A matrix with the same number of rows and columns.
    🔗
    Identity Matrix
    🔗
    A square matrix whose diagonal entries are equal to \(1\) and whose off-diagonal entries are equal to \(0\text{.}\) It is denoted by \(I\) or, if the size matters, by \(I_n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Diagonal Matrix
    🔗
    A square matrix whose off-diagonal entries are all zero.
    🔗
    Triangular Matrix
    🔗
    A square matrix is called upper triangular if all entries below the main diagonal are zero, and lower triangular if all entries above the main diagonal are zero.
    🔗
    Symmetric Matrix
    🔗
    A square matrix \(A\) satisfying \(A^T=A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Skew-Symmetric Matrix
    🔗
    A square matrix \(A\) satisfying \(A^T=-A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Example 1.1.36. Examples of Special Matrices.
    The matrices
    \begin{equation*} 0= \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad I_2= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad D= \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & -3 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}
    are a zero matrix, an identity matrix, and a diagonal matrix, respectively.
    🔗
    Moreover,
    \begin{equation*} U= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}
    is upper triangular, and
    \begin{equation*} S= \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 5 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}
    is symmetric.
    🔗
    🔗
    Theorem 1.1.37. Laws of Matrix Arithmetic.
    Let \(A,B,C\) be matrices of suitable sizes, and let \(\lambda \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Then the following rules hold.
    🔗
    • Addition: \(A+B=B+A\) and \((A+B)+C=A+(B+C)\text{,}\) provided the matrices have the same size.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Neutral and Inverse Elements: \(A+0=A\) and \(A+(-A)=0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Associativity of Multiplication: \((AB)C=A(BC)\text{,}\) whenever all products are defined.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Identity Matrix: \(AI=A\) and \(IA=A\text{,}\) where \(I\) is the identity matrix of suitable size.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Distributivity: \(A(B+C)=AB+AC\) and \((A+B)C=AC+BC\text{,}\) whenever the products are defined.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Compatibility with Scalars: \(\lambda(AB)=(\lambda A)B=A(\lambda B)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Remark 1.1.38. Matrix Multiplication Is Not Commutative.
    In general, matrix multiplication is not commutative. This means that even if both products \(AB\) and \(BA\) are defined, they need not be equal.
    🔗
    🔗
    Example 1.1.39. A Counterexample to Commutativity.
    Let
    \begin{equation*} A= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad B= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Then
    \begin{equation*} AB= \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad BA= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Hence, \(AB \neq BA\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsubsection 1.1.2.3 Matrices Define Linear Maps

    Every matrix defines a map that sends input vectors to output vectors by matrix-vector multiplication.
    🔗
    Definition 1.1.40. Linear Map Defined by a Matrix.
    Let \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\text{.}\) The map
    \begin{equation*} \varphi_A : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m, \qquad \mathbf{v} \mapsto A\mathbf{v} \end{equation*}
    is called the linear map defined by \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Thus, matrices do not merely store numbers. They also describe transformations of vectors.
    🔗
    Example 1.1.41. Rotation in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) as a Linear Map.
    A rotation about the origin through an angle \(\alpha\) is described by the matrix
    \begin{equation*} A= \begin{pmatrix} \cos\alpha & -\sin\alpha \\ \sin\alpha & \cos\alpha \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    For example, if \(\alpha=\frac{\pi}{2}\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} A= \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Applying this matrix to the vector \(\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}\) gives
    \begin{equation*} A \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Hence, the vector is rotated by \(90^\circ\) counterclockwise.
    🔗
    🔗
    Example 1.1.42. The Columns of a Matrix Determine the Linear Map.
    Consider
    \begin{equation*} A= \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Its columns are
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{a}_1= \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{a}_2= \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    For a vector
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{x}= \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{pmatrix}, \end{equation*}
    we have
    \begin{equation*} A\mathbf{x}=x_1\mathbf{a}_1+x_2\mathbf{a}_2. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This shows that the image of \(\mathbf{x}\) under the linear map defined by \(A\) is obtained as a linear combination of the columns of \(A\text{.}\)
